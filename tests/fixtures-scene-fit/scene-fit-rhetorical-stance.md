# Survey — scene-fit-rhetorical-stance (hermetic fixture)

Mode: ex
Subject: The free-will debate — compatibilism as a rhetorical position

This is a synthetic survey used by `docent hermetic-scene-fit` to verify the
mapper pulls the rhetorical primitives (`epigraph`, `objection`) when the
subject is a contested topic whose argumentation needs rhetorical moves.

## 0. Content boundary

The film argues for compatibilism — the position that free will and
determinism are compatible. Hard incompatibilism, libertarian free will
proper, and the neuroscientific evidence are out of scope. The film
defends ONE position on a genuinely contested topic.

## 1. Triage — the rhetorical scaffolding

This is a contested topic. The film cannot present compatibilism as a
neutral exposition; it must announce its commitment AND answer the
strongest counter-argument. Two rhetorical moves carry the argument:

- **Epigraph** — the film opens with a cited authority: Hume's claim that
  "by liberty, then, we can only mean a power of acting or not acting,
  according to the determinations of the will." Hume is the named tradition
  the film argues with, not from. A cited authority opens the film and the
  rest of the argument argues with it.
- **Objection** — Galen Strawson's basic argument has been challenged in
  the actual literature and the film must answer it. The anticipated
  counter-argument: "to be truly responsible, one must be causa sui — the
  ultimate cause of oneself — and nobody can be that." The film cannot
  ignore this; it must present the steelman, then refute it: responsibility
  does not require ultimate origination, only the right kind of causal
  history.

## 2. What it is

A philosophy explainer in the argumentative mode. The film's stance is
what's at issue — neutral exposition would betray the subject. The
rhetorical primitives carry the editorial commitment.

## 3. The hard parts

- **The misconception to kill** — compatibilism is sometimes dismissed as
  "wishy-washy". The film must show it is a sharp position with bite.
- **The refutation pattern** — the standard objection that compatibilism
  is "the only free will worth wanting" (Dennett) is itself an objection
  the film should anticipate, not endorse.

## 4. Verdict

A film on this topic without an epigraph reads as ungrounded; without an
objection it reads as evasive.
