# Survey — scene-fit-temporal (hermetic fixture)

Mode: ex
Subject: The fifty-year arc of the AI winter — dated milestones, 1956 → 2012

This is a synthetic survey used by `docent hermetic-scene-fit` to verify the
mapper pulls the `timeline` primitive when the argument hinges on dated
milestones whose spacing matters.

## 0. Content boundary

The film covers six dated milestones on a real time axis: the Dartmouth
Workshop (1956), the Lighthill report (1973), the LISP machine bust (1987),
the Deep Blue chess match (1997), the ImageNet release (2009), and the
AlexNet result (2012). The intellectual content of each milestone is out of
scope — the GAPS between them are the argument.

## 1. Triage — the load-bearing gaps

Three time gaps carry the entire argument:

- 17 years between Dartmouth (1956) and Lighthill (1973) — the first
  optimism cycle.
- 10 years between Lighthill (1973) and the LISP boom (~1983) — the funding
  recovery.
- 23 years between Deep Blue (1997) and the modern era (~2020) — the long
  walk through unfashionable methods.

The argument needs a chronological date axis with the proportional distance
between events visible on screen. An ordinal "first, then, then" cannot
encode that the 17-year gap and the 23-year gap are NOT the same length.

## 2. What it is

A historical record of AI as a discipline, told as the gaps between funding
cycles rather than the breakthroughs themselves. The timeline shape is
load-bearing: the milestone dates are exact, not placeholders.

## 3. The hard parts

- **Where the dates contest the narrative** — the "winter" framing implies
  silence, but the survey shows steady academic publication throughout.
  The winter was a funding winter, not a research winter.

## 4. Verdict

The arc of an idea from publication to consensus — the long, dated wait —
is the most honest way to tell this history.
