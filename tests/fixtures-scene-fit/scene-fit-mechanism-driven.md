# Survey — scene-fit-mechanism-driven (hermetic fixture)

Mode: ex
Subject: How a four-stroke internal combustion engine cycle works

This is a synthetic survey used by `docent hermetic-scene-fit` to verify the
mapper pulls the `mechanism` primitive when the subject is a working motion
that must be shown in continuous motion.

## 0. Content boundary

The film covers one cylinder of a four-stroke gasoline engine across one
full cycle — intake, compression, power, exhaust. Multi-cylinder timing,
diesel, and electric powertrains are out of scope.

## 1. Triage — the load-bearing mechanism

The argument is HOW it operates, not WHAT it is. The four-stroke engine
cycle is a working motion that cycles through phases in a fixed order:

1. Intake — piston down, intake valve open, mixture drawn in.
2. Compression — both valves closed, piston up, mixture compressed.
3. Power — spark, mixture ignites, piston driven down.
4. Exhaust — exhaust valve open, piston up, spent gas pushed out.

This is an animated mechanism. A static diagram cannot carry the argument
because the engine IS its cycle — the parts arranged in continuous motion
matter as much as the parts themselves. The piston iterates through the
four phases in a closed cycle; the camshaft synchronizes the valves with
the piston position.

## 2. What it is

A state cycle of four named phases. The cycle iterates indefinitely while
the engine runs. The film must show the motion to teach it; a freeze-frame
at the "power" phase lets the narration call out what's happening, but the
default is motion.

## 3. The hard parts

- **The misconception** — students often think the spark "pushes" the
  piston. It does not; the *expanding combustion gas* does. The film must
  freeze on the power phase and annotate the gas pressure, not the spark.
- **Where the cycle strains** — at low RPM the timing margin between
  phases is wide; at high RPM the valves can "float" and the cycle phase
  ordering breaks down.

## 4. Verdict

The motion IS the argument; a still diagram of an engine cycle has been
the standard teaching failure for a hundred years.
