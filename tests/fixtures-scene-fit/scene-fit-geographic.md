# Survey — scene-fit-geographic (hermetic fixture)

Mode: ex
Subject: The 1854 Broad Street cholera outbreak — John Snow's map

This is a synthetic survey used by `docent hermetic-scene-fit` to verify the
mapper pulls the `map` primitive when geography is load-bearing.

## 0. Content boundary

The film covers Snow's epidemiology of the Soho outbreak — the geographic
distribution of cholera deaths around the Broad Street pump. Miasma theory
and the broader Victorian public-health context are out of scope.

## 1. Triage — the load-bearing geography

The argument is spatial: where the cholera deaths occurred matters. Snow's
contribution was plotting fatality counts as dots on a Soho street map and
noticing the geographic clustering around one well. The transmission paths
followed the local proximity to a single contaminated water source.

This was the first formal use of geography as evidence in epidemiology.
The regional topology of the outbreak — where the deaths *were* — defeated
the prevailing miasma theory because the airborne-miasma hypothesis predicted
a different spatial distribution.

## 2. What it is

A map-based proof. Each death is a dot whose position on the street map is
the data. The Broad Street pump's location is the inferred cause, named on
the map. The regions of the outbreak follow the pump's catchment, not the
prevailing wind.

## 3. The hard parts

- **Where the spatial story breaks** — the brewery on Broad Street had zero
  cases (the workers drank beer, not water). The map has to explain the
  absence as well as the presence.
- **Transmission paths and proximity** — the dots cluster within a five-minute
  walking radius of the pump, falling off sharply at the edges of that region.

## 4. Verdict

The map is the argument; a non-spatial visualization could not have made
Snow's case to the Board of Health.
