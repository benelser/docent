## 0. Content boundary

The film covers the four stanzas of the poem — sixteen lines. Frost's letters
about the poem and the broader literary context are out of scope. This is a
close reading of one short poem, not a survey of his work.

## 1. Triage — the load-bearing line

The poem turns on its repeated final line: "And miles to go before I sleep."
Read once it is a literal statement; read twice, with the woods reframed,
it becomes a confession about deferred rest. The close reading interrogates
this doubling — how a single line, repeated, becomes its own metaphor.

The first two stanzas (the woods, the horse, the bells) are scaffolding; the
turn happens in the third stanza when the speaker stops noticing the
landscape and starts naming the obligation that pulls him onward.

## 2. What the idea is

The poem is an essay in compression: a narrative about a stopped traveler
that is also a meditation on the cost of obligation. The literary critic's
question — "what does the snow MEAN?" — is the wrong question. The snow is
a setting; the *attention to the snow* is the argument.

## 3. The hard parts

- **The misconception to kill** — the reading that calls the woods a
  symbol of death. Frost himself dismissed this; the poem's metaphor is
  about pause, not ending. A film that endorses the death-reading without
  qualifying it is repeating a high-school commonplace.
- **Where the reading breaks** — the repeated final line. A claim that
  "repetition deepens meaning" is unfalsifiable. The strong reading commits
  to a specific shift between the first and second utterance: the first is
  about the literal miles, the second about the obligation.

## 4. Is the claim earned

The reading is grounded in the prose rhythm of the poem itself — Frost's own
prosody. The film should quote the lines, not summarize them; the evidence is
the text.

## 7. The Big Idea

The line repeats because the speaker has heard himself say it — and learned
something. The first utterance ends a stanza; the second ends a life's
project.

## 8. Verdict

Sound with caveats — the close reading commits to one of several legitimate
readings; the film should name the others it set aside, not pretend the
poem has only one meaning.
