# Treatment — Linear Algebra

- **Subject** — Linear algebra as Module 1 of an applied deep-learning course teaches it: a small base of objects and one keystone operation.
- **Audience** — A learner who has arithmetic and the idea of an ordered list of numbers, but no prior linear algebra.
- **Angle** — The film tests one claim: that linear algebra is a small family of objects plus a single keystone operation, and every bigger operation is that keystone restacked — then asks where the claim holds and where it quietly stops.
- **Estimated length** — About eight minutes — eight short parts, roughly a minute each.

## The outline — eight parts

1. **The bag of formulas** — Open on the picture most learners arrive with — linear algebra as a pile of unrelated formulas, each memorised on its own — so the film has a wrong idea to dismantle and a reason to exist.

2. **One family of objects** — Introduce the whole cast — a single number, a list, a grid, a stack of grids — and show they are one family that differs only in how many directions it counts along, not four separate things to learn.

3. **The keystone, worked by hand** — Work the dot product on real numbers — multiply two short lists term by term, add the results, reach one total — so the operation the rest of the film rests on is concrete before any weight is placed on it.

4. **A matrix leads a double life** — Show that a matrix is two things at once, a plain table of data and a machine that reshapes space, because that double identity is the hinge the whole compounding claim turns on.

5. **Every operation, the keystone restacked** — Prove the film's central claim by building it: measuring a vector's length, comparing two vectors' direction, and multiplying matrices all turn out to be the dot product used again and again.

6. **Where the spine bends** — Name honestly where "complexity is just simplicity stacked" stops being true — stacking these simple steps collapses back into one step instead of compounding, un-doing a transformation cannot be built this way at all, and the whole story holds only while lines stay straight and the origin stays fixed.

7. **The keystone's unpaid debt** — Surface the module's biggest weak spot: the dot product's deeper meaning — why it measures whether two vectors point the same way — is leaned on a page later but never actually explained, so the keystone is taught as a recipe with its reason still owed.

8. **The verdict — sound, with caveats** — Land an honest scorecard: the compounding idea is the right spine and genuinely pays off, but it is only the forward half of the subject, and the most interesting parts of what comes next live in exactly what it leaves out.

## Open choices

Three real forks below. The recommendation is mine; the decision is yours.

**1. How hard should the verdict land?**
The survey's read is "sound, with caveats" — and the film can weight those two halves differently.
- **A.** Lead with the strength: the spine holds, and the gaps are honest previews of lessons still to come.
- **B.** Balanced: give the payoff and the unpaid debt equal weight.
- **C.** Lead with the gap: the module's keystone is taught as a recipe without its reason, and say so first.
Trade: A is generous to a knowledge base that is openly mid-construction; C is the sharper, more useful review but risks reading as harsh on unfinished work; B sits between.
*Recommend B* — the survey itself holds both truths at once, and the viewer deserves the same. Yours to override.

**2. Should "Where the spine bends" go broad or deep?**
There are three places the compounding claim stops: stacked steps collapsing into one, the un-doable inverse, and the straight-line assumption.
- **A.** Broad: touch all three lightly in one part.
- **B.** Deep: spend the part almost entirely on the collapse — stacking these steps adds no real power without something non-linear — and name the other two in a line.
Trade: A shows the full boundary of the claim; B goes deep on the one limit that matters most for where the course is headed and lands harder, at the cost of two real caveats getting only a mention.
*Recommend B* — the collapse is the most counterintuitive of the three and the most load-bearing for deep learning. Yours to override.

**3. Does the road-not-taken get its own moment?**
The wiki teaches arithmetic-first; a well-known rival teaches geometry-first — intuition and pictures before any computation.
- **A.** Give it a dedicated part contrasting the two ways in.
- **B.** Keep it to a single line inside the verdict.
Trade: A buys a richer picture of a real design decision, but adds a part — a slightly longer film, or trading out the lightest part of the spine; B keeps the film tight on its own claim.
*Recommend B* — the film's job is to test the wiki's own thesis, not to referee a teaching debate; one honest line carries it. Yours to override.
