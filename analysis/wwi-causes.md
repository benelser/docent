# Survey — Causes of World War I (explainer mode)

**Subject.** The Wikipedia page `Causes of World War I`, fetched to
`analysis/wwi-causes.source.md`, treated as an argument about causation rather
than as a full diplomatic history.

**Mode.** Explainer (`ex`). The film is `films/wwi-causes.json`.

---

## 0. Content boundary — the explainable unit

The source page is encyclopedic. It covers immediate causes, alliance
realignment, colonial crises, domestic politics, imperialism, Social Darwinism,
arms races, military planning, and historiography. That is too much for one
film.

**The explainable unit chosen:** the page's central corrective to the schoolbook
myth that World War I was "caused by the assassination of Franz Ferdinand."
More precisely: **the war came from a short-term Balkan crisis landing inside a
European system already loaded with rival alignments, military planning, and
fear**. The page states that directly in the introduction by splitting the
question into long-term causes and short-term July 1914 decisions, then later
endorses the view that the outbreak was "massively overdetermined" rather than
the result of one master cause (source lines 332-338, 879-889).

- **In scope:** the introduction's long-term / short-term split; the July
  Crisis; the alliance/alignment story; the arms-race and mobilization sections;
  the historiography summary that rejects single-cause history (source lines
  332-338, 361-362, 731-868, 879-889).
- **Named neighbours at the edge:** domestic political factors, imperialism,
  and Social Darwinism. They support the atmosphere of rivalry and fatalism, but
  the film will name them as pressure, not center them as separate arguments
  (source lines 620-724).
- **Out of scope:** a country-by-country diplomatic brief; the full blame debate
  in the separate historiography page; detailed Balkan ethnography; the war's
  conduct after August 1914; postwar aftermath.
- **Prerequisites assumed of the viewer:** a basic sense that Europe had great
  powers, empires, and armies; no prior expertise in diplomatic history.

## 1. Triage — load-bearing vs. mechanical

**Load-bearing**:
- The page's key move is to split causation into two layers: long-term
  structural tension and short-term July 1914 decisions (source lines 332-338).
- The assassination mattered because Vienna treated it as a casus belli and
  existential challenge, not because one murder mechanically forces a world war
  (source lines 351-353).
- The crisis widened because it ran through alignments, mobilization systems,
  and offensive war plans that limited diplomacy once the crisis accelerated
  (source lines 361-362, 852-868).
- The page's historiographical spine is anti-monocausal: multiple causes, any
  one or two sufficient to launch the war, yet not all states equally reckless
  (source lines 883-889).

**Mechanical**:
- The page's catalog of every Moroccan, Bosnian, Balkan, and Ottoman episode is
  valuable evidence, but the film does not need to retell each one chronologically.
- The long national subsections under domestic politics are support material,
  not the one explainable unit.
- The page's many side quotations and images help color the atmosphere, but
  they are not the causal spine.

The cut line: the film interrogates **why a local Balkan assassination widened
into general European war in 1914**. It does not attempt to rank every factor
ever proposed.

## 2. What the idea is / why it exists

In my own words: **the article argues that the causes of World War I were
layered, not singular**. The assassination was the trigger, but the trigger
worked only because Europe had already polarized into rival camps, had already
normalized militarized crisis management, and had already built mobilization
systems so rigid that diplomacy began to lose room to act once states started
moving (source lines 332-338, 731-868).

Why this idea exists: it corrects two familiar but bad models.

- First, the children's-textbook model: "Franz Ferdinand was shot, therefore
  Europe went to war."
- Second, the equally tidy domino model: two preformed alliance blocs clicked
  automatically into place.

The page exists to replace both with a harder claim: **a contingent decision
chain operated inside a preloaded continental system**.

## 3. The hard parts of the idea

- **Where it is counterintuitive.** More causes do not make the assassination
  less important; they explain why it could matter so much. The intuitive model
  says a huge war needs a huge cause. The page argues the reverse: if a system
  is already primed, a relatively local event can unleash it (source lines
  367-379, 731-868, 883-889).
- **The misconception it must kill.** The one-bullet story. The article also
  has to kill a subtler misconception: that Europe in 1914 was two perfectly
  automatic treaty machines. It explicitly warns that the Triple Entente was
  not a mutual-defense alliance but "a frame of mind," so alignment mattered,
  but not as a cartoonishly automatic switch (source lines 440-441).
- **Where it breaks.** The overdetermined account strains when the viewer asks
  for sharp blame. If many causes could have launched the war, why not shrug and
  say everyone caused it? The page itself resists that shrug by ending with a
  later scholarly consensus that Germany bore a larger share of responsibility,
  even though the search for one single cause is "not a helpful approach"
  (source lines 883-889).
- **The mechanism, not just the conclusion.** The mechanism is concrete in the
  source: Vienna sees the murder as a casus belli; Berlin offers the blank
  cheque; France reaffirms Russia; mobilization plans reward speed and punish
  hesitation; partial Russian mobilization collapses into general mobilization;
  German planning assumes a two-front war and rapid western attack (source lines
  353, 361-362, 852-868). The film should show that sequence, not just say
  "tensions were high."

## 4. Is the claim earned

- **Demonstrated.** The source demonstrates the short-term mechanism reasonably
  well. It explains why Austria-Hungary interpreted the assassination as a
  challenge to imperial survival, details the blank cheque, and shows how
  mobilization timetables narrowed diplomacy once the crisis accelerated (source
  lines 351-353, 361-362, 852-868).
- **Cited.** The page defers major interpretive claims to historians such as
  Christopher Clark, David Stevenson, David Herrmann, Paul Schroeder, and
  Annika Mombauer. Those citations are load-bearing because they support the
  article's central anti-monocausal argument and its later caveat about German
  responsibility (source lines 450, 473-475, 736, 866-868, 879-889).
- **Asserted or weakly ranked.** The page is less strong when it moves into the
  long catalog of structural causes. It names imperial closure, Social
  Darwinism, and many diplomatic crises, but often as plausible contributing
  pressures rather than as carefully ranked causes. Some surrounding claims are
  explicitly marked `[citation needed]`, which is a yellow flag for any film
  tempted to treat every listed factor as equally earned (for example source
  lines 379, 442, 492-494, 747).

The central claim is therefore **earned enough to explain**, but not precise
enough to pretend the page has settled the causation debate. Its strongest
evidence is the immediate July mechanism and the historiographical warning
against single-cause history.

## 5. The scope of the claim — where the idea does not apply

The claim holds when the question is: **why did a Balkan crisis become a general
European war in 1914?**

It does not fully answer:

- **Who alone caused the war?** The page explicitly says consensus remains
  elusive and that a single main cause is the wrong frame (source lines 338,
  883-889).
- **Did alliances automatically force war?** No. The source complicates that by
  stating the Triple Entente was not a mutual-defense alliance and that Britain
  still retained policy freedom in 1914 (source lines 440-441).
- **Which long-term factor mattered most?** The page lists imperialism,
  nationalism, security dilemmas, war plans, domestic politics, and ideology,
  but it does not decisively weight them against one another. That is a boundary
  of the article, not merely of the film.

So the safe scope sentence is: **true as an explanation of escalation, weak as a
single-culprit verdict, and incomplete as a ranking of every deep cause**.

## 6. The competing explanation

The strongest rival explanation is a **single-culprit account**, especially the
claim that Germany deliberately sought a European war and should sit at the
center of causation. The page takes that view seriously. In historiography, it
notes Fischer's argument about German leaders deliberately seeking war, then
records a later consensus that Germany's share of responsibility was larger than
that of the other great powers (source lines 879-889).

Why the page still chooses the broader, overdetermined account:

- It better explains **mechanism**: how a local assassination widened into
  continental war through alignments, mobilization, misperception, and prior
  crises.
- It fits the source's own conclusion that multiple causes, any one or two
  sufficient, were simultaneously live (source lines 883-889).

What that choice costs:

- It risks blurring agency into atmosphere.
- It can sound like "everything mattered," which is one step away from "nothing
  decisive happened."
- Without the historiography caveat about Germany's larger share, the systemic
  account can become an evasion of responsibility.

The film should therefore choose **systemic escalation over monocausal blame**,
but say plainly that this choice costs a cleaner moral story.

## 7. Verdict inputs — a takeaway that adjudicates

- **Disposition.** Sound with caveats. The article is right to reject the
  assassination-only myth and right to insist on layered causation. Its best
  explanatory move is the splice between long-term structures and the July
  decision chain (source lines 332-338, 361-362, 852-868).
- **The single biggest weak point.** The page's encyclopedic breadth makes
  overdetermination easy to state and hard to rank. It can flatten the
  difference between background pressure and decisive late-July risk-taking.
- **What to tell the viewer to carry away.** The assassination was the spark,
  not the fuel. Europe had already built a war-prone system; July 1914 turned
  that system from dangerous to actual. But stay skeptical of any version of
  "many causes" that washes out agency entirely: the source itself still lands
  on a larger German share of responsibility (source lines 887-889).
